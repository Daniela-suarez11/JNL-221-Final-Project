How Has NYC Transit Changed Since The Pandemic?

NYC transit numbers changed drastically following the COVID-19 pandemic

By: Daniela Suarez

May 5, 2025 at 10:00 p.m. EST

New York residents wait for their train (jentzphoto /Alamy Stock Photo)

The way New Yorkers navigate the city has seen a dramatic shift since the onset of the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. What once revolved around a traditional Monday to Friday rush hour routine has evolved into a more fluid pattern, marked by growing weekend activity and uneven weekday recovery. This shift is evident in public transit ridership data collected by the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA), suggesting not just a change in transportation habits but in how city life itself is unfolding.

According to data sourced from the New York State Open Data Portal , the MTA has tracked daily ridership across five main systems from 2020 to 2025: subways, buses, the Long Island Rail Road (LIRR), the Metro-North Railroad, and the Staten Island Railway. This dataset includes daily totals and comparisons to pre-pandemic levels. Analysis of the data reveals major declines in ridership during the height of the pandemic and a long, uneven recovery that continues into the present.

A Steep Decline in 2020

In March and April 2020, ridership across all systems dropped dramatically. Subway use fell from more than 60 million weekday rides to fewer than 10 million. Bus ridership reached near zero on weekends between April and August, while commuter systems like the LIRR and Metro-North flatlined. Even by the end of 2020, weekday subway use only rebounded to around 35 million. The bus system showed some recovery, starting a steady incline in August and gaining momentum into the fall.

Line graph showing 2020 weekday ridership data (MTA).

Weekend numbers followed a similar pattern. Though subway usage remained above 2 million, buses dropped to near zero and did not show consistent recovery until August. Staten Island Railway, LIRR, and Metro-North remained almost entirely flat during this period, only averaging about 2,000 monthly riders.

Line graph showing 2020 weekend ridership data (MTA).

Gradual Weekend Recovery

By 2021, ridership patterns began to stabilize, particularly on weekends. Subway usage on weekends rose steadily, peaking at about 18 million in October. Bus usage held around 5 million monthly riders. Metro-North and LIRR continued to hover at low levels or near zero for weekends. Weekday subway rides increased as well, growing from 30 million early in the year to 67 million in November.

Line graph showing 2021 weekday ridership data (MTA).

This trend continued into 2022. Weekend subway ridership climbed to 20 million by October, and weekday subway numbers moved from 50 million to a plateau around 74 million by December. Buses showed consistency in both weekday and weekend numbers, while LIRR and Metro-North began to recover slightly, now averaging nearly 1 million monthly riders. The Staten Island Railway remained at very low usage levels across all months.

Remote Work and Changing Commutes

These ridership shifts align with the rise of remote and hybrid work arrangements. While the dataset does not track employment status or remote work percentages, the flattening of traditional weekday peaks and rise in weekend activity suggest that fewer New Yorkers are commuting five days a week.

Commuter Laura Suarez, who moved back into the city in late 2022 after working remotely for more than a year said, “I didn’t need to worry about the train until I started going in a few times a week. Even then, I’d walk to work more often. I only noticed transit crowding again on weekends."

By 2023, subway weekend ridership peaked at 22 million in December. Bus usage remained steady around 6 million. Weekday subway use climbed steadily throughout the year, peaking at 86 million in May. Despite this growth, weekday numbers still fell short of pre-pandemic peaks.

2024 and the Weekend Boom

The latest available data for 2024 shows subway weekday ridership hitting a high of 93 million in October. Bus use stayed consistent at 30 million per month. LIRR and Metro-North reached up to 5 million rides monthly, while the Staten Island Railway remained low but stable.

What stands out, however, is weekend activity. Subway weekend rides peaked in May at 22 million, and bus usage on weekends held at 6 to 7 million. This surge suggests that public transit has become a more essential part of how New Yorkers spend their leisure time; visiting friends, dining out, and exploring the city.

What This Can and Can't Tell Us

The MTA dataset clearly tracks transit usage patterns, but it has limits. It does not include rider demographics, employment details, or motivations for travel. Without data on how many New Yorkers work from home, commute occasionally, or have shifted to fully in-person jobs, it is difficult to draw definitive conclusions about causation.

Still, the patterns are suggestive. The rise in weekend ridership may reflect renewed tourism, growth in local events and hospitality industries, and the reactivation of public life. For example, ridership spikes in December on commuter lines may correlate with holiday shopping and seasonal events.

Looking Forward

To further understand these changes, transit experts recommend pairing ridership data with employment and lifestyle data, such as office occupancy rates, real estate trends, or retail spending. Such integration could help the MTA and city planners adapt service schedules, fare models, and infrastructure investments to better meet current demands.

For commuters, the data offers insights into how to navigate the city efficiently. Fewer riders during weekday off-peak hours might encourage people to shift their schedules. Meanwhile, those traveling on weekends may face growing crowds as leisure activity surges.

"Transit isn’t just about getting to work anymore," said Suarez. "It’s part of how we are able to live in the city again."
